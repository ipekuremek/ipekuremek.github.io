Artificial Intelligence and the Problem of Knowledge

Recently, a new concept has entered our lives: Artificial Intelligence. Artificial intelligence, which everyone keeps talking about, has actually been around in our lives for quite some time, only we haven't been very aware of it. With applications like ChatGPT, now available to the public, artificial intelligence has become a bit more visible. The open-ended issue of artificial intelligence also raises many questions in people's minds: "Can artificial intelligence be superior to humans? Will it destroy us in the future?"

I can add a few things to these questions brought to a philosophical standpoint. First of all, artificial intelligence is a technology that operates entirely on human data. There are a lot of other systems underneath it, which we don't need to go into detail. In Machine Learning, a subfield of artificial intelligence that I frequently use in my own projects, you teach various data sets to the AI. It makes a probability plan, calculating the outcome you want to achieve with real-time data that has a high probability. So, the model you create goes through a decision-making process by calculating the probabilities of possible situations. The more data, the better the results. The simplest example could be an autonomous vehicle. You must load thousands of car data into the autonomous vehicle so that it detects the car in front and applies the brakes, increasing the likelihood of detection and braking, i.e., stopping when it sees an object. You also need to teach it about a pink car, a hatchback car, a sedan car, a car with a damaged bumper, etc., so it can recognize cars of different types. Thus, artificial intelligence always does the action humans want it to do with the data provided by humans. It does not yet have its own questioning mechanism. Like a parrot, you teach it something, let's say to say "Hello," but the parrot doesn't even know that it means hello; it's just repeating.

Of course, there are new technologies available. For example, artificial neural networks are designed and programmed by humans and trained through algorithms. Since they are designed to mimic the neural networks in the human brain, they have the potential for self-improvement in addition to Machine Learning. This system is already working so well that artificial intelligence , can even beat the world's best chess player. There is even an AI that has beaten the world's best Go players in a complex game like Go. (See: DeepMind's AlphaGo and IBM's Deep Blue.) However, we are still talking about algorithms and actions created by "humans."

So, can artificial intelligence distinguish between good and evil? If you showed it the recent climate crises of the world, children emaciated from hunger, and species becoming extinct, it might reach the assumption that these were caused by humans. But to say that humans are "bad" for doing this would only be possible if a human provided it with that input. At least with the current artificial intelligence technologies. For artificial intelligence to have such ethical questioning, it would need a vast amount of data sets, similar to the social data that form the moral personality possessed by humans. In this case, we might think that artificial intelligence is not much different from humans since almost all of a person's personality is formed by society. The judgment mechanism for most people is religion, judicial authorities, or social rules. In fact, when we decide whether something is good or bad, we make inferences from thousands, even millions of data sets, but especially a majority of people at a certain intellectual level act according to their conscience and ethical questioning. Otherwise, as Socrates said, "Is man inherently good?". According to him, good knowledge leads to a moral life. A virtuous life is only possible with knowledge, but not just any knowledge, the knowledge that leads to the good. Socrates' method is often known as midwifery because it allows the person opposite to reach good knowledge by thinking for themselves. So maybe this method leads us to what we call the inner conscience by refuting the data sets imposed on us by society.

Let's approach knowledge from another angle: Is there really such a thing as knowledge? Can we truly see the essence of the knowledge perceived through everyone's own lens? Everything we learn is reflected through the lens of an individual, and even if we experience the same event at the same time with someone, we see that we make entirely different inferences when compared to that person. So, when the transmission of the knowledge we learn is dubious, even possibly impossible, from the teacher to us in its pure form, we also have our own preconceptions! In the school of Skepticism, the last of the Hellenistic philosophical schools, it is said that we can only know about the appearance of things and can never know their true form. Therefore, suspension of judgment - epoche - is necessary. Approaching without judgment will lead to happiness. Is knowledge really possible in such a case? According to the Sophists, knowledge is relative, and therefore, there is no such thing as truth. A societal truth is not possible. But Socrates thinks differently from the Sophists; according to him, good knowledge and truth exist and must be sought. The possibility of knowledge in Indian philosophy and yoga is approached from a completely different perspective. In yoga, the possibility of knowledge involves an in-depth examination of the nature of the mind and the perception of reality. Cleansing the mind from illusions - maya - and bringing it to a state of pure consciousness is essential. Thus, the process of attaining knowledge in Indian philosophy can be possible with a journey into one's inner world and perceiving the nature of reality. Inferences from many different schools still lead me back to that initial problem of knowledge and truth. Perhaps, like the first piece of data given to artificial intelligence, the famous apple eaten by Adam is the truth.
